About

Driven to Expand the Boundaries of Computer Vision in Medical Research and Practice

I believe that the future of healthcare lies in advancing automated diagnosis and treatment, both in research and clinical practice. Imagine a world where disease prediction, diagnosis, and medical operations are supported by a collaborative system between physicians and AI. Such integration could lead to faster, more accurate, and more cost-effective care. However, achieving this vision requires overcoming several major challenges, which I categorize into two domains: Soft barriers, which include pure AI, robotics, and computational scientific challenges, as well as designing reliable clinical AI-driven systems; and hard barriers, which refer to infrastructure-level needs such as fully open-access database organizations and interdisciplinary R&D ecosystems to reduce development costs. To play my role, I began researching medical signal processing and computer vision 27 months ago. Since then, I’ve focused on developing an algorithm for PPG signals and designing deep learning models for the classification and segmentation of ICH in head CT scans—at Zistel and APAC Research Group, respectively.

Abstract:

Intracranial hemorrhages (ICHs) pose a critical medical challenge with a high mortality rate, necessitating timely and accurate diagnosis. This study focuses on enhancing the performance of deep learning models for classifying brain hemorrhages in CT scan images using the PhysioNet dataset. A grid search methodology was employed to tune hyperparameters, particularly addressing the imbalance between ICH and healthy slices. The study utilized ResNet50 for hyperparameter tuning, achieving significant improvements in sensitivity and overall performance through undersampling, bootstrapping, augmentation, and weighted loss techniques. The ResNet50 model demonstrated remarkable performance at the slice-level scope, achieving a sensitivity of 0.94, a specificity of 0.91, an F1 score of 0.64, and an accuracy of 0.91. It outperformed the literature in both sensitivity and accuracy, despite utilizing a smaller and more constrained dataset. At the patient-level scope, our approach achieved even higher metrics with a sensitivity of 1.00, a specificity of 0.80, an F1 score of 0.86, and an accuracy of 0.88, surpassing the literature across all reported metrics.

Abstract:

Photoplethysmogram (PPG) signals have become increasingly valuable for monitoring various healthcare factors, such as heart rate, blood pressure, arrhythmia, stress levels, and arterial flexibility. However, the accurate analysis of PPG signals is often hindered by the presence of artifacts and noise, which can lead to erroneous interpretations. This study proposes a novel approach for preprocessing and assessing the quality of individual beats in PPG signals to facilitate subsequent analysis. A new and robust algorithm was developed to detect the signal valleys, which were used to identify each beat individually. This valley detection method demonstrated high performance, achieving a sensitivity, positive predictive value, and F1 score of 99.33%, 99.52%, and 99.43%, respectively. Several statistical and time-domain features were extracted from each beat to distinguish good-quality beats from bad ones, considering both accuracy and computational complexity. The XGBoost machine learning model, utilizing five selected features, classified the input signals as either good or bad with an accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity of 92.11%, 94.10%, and 90.11%, respectively. The proposed approach was evaluated using PPG signals from the publicly available MIMIC III clinical database, which contains physiological data from intensive care unit (ICU) patients. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in improving the reliability and accuracy of PPG signal analysis compared to commonly used methods, particularly in terms of valley detection and quality assessment.

Abstract:

Wireless Sensor Networks (WSN) nodes are extensively applied across various applications. One of the main challenges of WSN is the throughput that can be directly affected by the energy consumption of nodes. Consumption of nodes can be analyzed discretely or continuously; however, recent studies have focused on discrete energy analysis. In discretization-based methods, energy is divided into various ranges which causes the reduction of throughput compared to the cases where the energy is analyzed continuously. Addressing this issue requires an effective approach, so this study presents a mechanism relying on Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which integrates Reinforcement Learning (RL) with Deep Learning (DL) techniques. This method makes the throughput more efficient by transmitting maximum data from individual sensor nodes to the base station, ensuring continuous energy availability. By maintaining energy availability and adapting dynamically to environmental changes, the approach ensures sustained and efficient data flow, thus overcoming traditional limitations. The primary goal of this method is to empower each sensor node to determine the best action in order to enhance throughput based on its energy value. Simulation outcomes indicate a 11.79% enhancement in throughput over existing advanced techniques. This approach dynamically adapts to varying energy conditions, providing a more precise adjustment of sensor node actions compared to traditional methods, thus enhancing the network’s overall efficiency.

Abstract:

Timely diagnosis of Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH) on Computed Tomography (CT) scans remains a clinical priority, yet the development of robust Artificial Intelligence (AI) solutions is still hindered by fragmented public data. To close this gap, we introduce Hemorica, a publicly available collection of 327 head-CT examinations acquired between 2012 and 2024. Each scan has been exhaustively annotated for five ICH sub-types—epidural (EPH), subdural (SDH), subarachnoid (SAH), intraparenchymal (IPH), and intraventricular (IVH)— yielding patient-wise and slice-wise classification labels, subtype-specific bounding boxes, two-dimensional pixel masks and three-dimensional voxel masks. A double-reading workflow, preceded by a pilot consensus phase and supported by neurosurgeon adjudication, maintained low inter-rater variability. Comprehensive statistical analysis confirms the clinical realism of the dataset. To establish reference baselines, standard convolutional and transformer architectures were fine-tuned for binary slice classification and hemorrhage segmentation. With only minimal fine-tuning, lightweight models such as ResNet-18 achieved an F1 score of 0.875 in classification, whereas a U-Net with an EfficientNet-B5 encoder reached a Dice score of 0.832 for lesion segmentation—figures that validate both the quality of the annotations and the sufficiency of the sample size. Hemorica therefore offers a unified, fine-grained benchmark that supports multi-task and curriculum learning, facilitates transfer to larger but weakly labelled cohorts, and facilitates the process of designing an AI-based assistant for ICH detection and quantification systems.
